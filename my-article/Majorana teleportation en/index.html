Teleportation by a Majorana Medium

Revisit the non-locality of Majorana zero modes and teleportation: Bogoliubov-de Gennes equation based treatment

Electron Teleportation via Majorana Bound States in a Mesoscopic Superconductor

Andreev-Tunneling, Coulomb Blockade, and Resonant Transport of Non-Local Spin-Entangled Electrons

This type of article highlights a significant effect: the real teleportation of electrons between the two ends of a nanowire. However, as far as I know, there is still no experimental evidence for the non-locality of Majorana modes?

You have pointed out a key controversy in contemporary quantum physics: despite the well-developed theoretical understanding of Majorana zero modes' non-locality and their role in quantum teleportation, experimental evidence for this non-locality remains inconclusive. The Majorana zero mode theory predicts a unique type of non-local quantum state: zero-energy Majorana modes at both ends of a nanowire. When an electron is injected at one end, its quantum state appears to "split" and manifest at the other end (while conserving fermion parity). Despite detailed theoretical research, experiments face the following challenges:

Candidate Experimental Platforms for Majorana Zero Modes

  • Semiconductor-superconductor heterostructure nanowires: For example, InSb or InAs nanowires coupled with an s-wave superconductor (e.g., Al).
  • Magnetic atom chains: Spin-controlled magnetic atoms deposited on a superconducting surface.
  • 2D topological insulator-superconductor interfaces: Such as quantum spin Hall edge states.

These platforms are designed to realize the theoretically predicted Majorana zero modes in experiments. Typically, superconductivity is induced in materials with strong spin-orbit coupling via proximity effects. The interplay of superconducting pairing, spin-orbit coupling, and magnetic fields can produce a topological superconducting state, thereby supporting Majorana zero modes at the boundaries or ends of nanowires. However, the observed signals are largely indirect evidence, such as:

  • Conductance peaks near zero bias in quantum dots or tunneling devices ("zero-bias conductance peaks").
  • Observations of quantum coherence oscillations or a topological gap under certain conditions.

To address this issue, possible solutions include:

  • Multi-terminal measurements and non-locality tests: Introducing multi-terminal probes (e.g., T-shaped structures) to verify the distribution of the Majorana wavefunction across both ends of the nanowire via interference and non-local correlations.
  • Testing non-Abelian statistics: Verifying whether the statistical behavior of Majorana states matches theoretical predictions through braiding operations.
  • Experimental extraction of topological invariants: Designing experiments to directly measure the topological characteristics of Majorana states, such as topological winding numbers.

Leaving aside the non-locality of Majorana modes, have superconducting nanowires, predicted and realized as a Kitaev model system, been experimentally confirmed and controlled?

Superconducting nanowire systems are considered experimental realizations of the Kitaev chain model, with core requirements including:

  • Strong spin-orbit coupling in the nanowire.
  • Proximity-induced superconductivity.
  • Topological gap opened by an external magnetic field.

Despite significant progress in achieving these conditions, some critical challenges remain, such as distinguishing Majorana states from Andreev bound states.

Superconducting nanowire systems have provided some experimental evidence supporting the Kitaev model, but the evidence is not yet definitive. The primary obstacles include distinguishing Majorana states from non-topological effects and how to stably and precisely manipulate Majorana states.

The Diversity of Andreev Bound States

Andreev bound states can form due to various physical mechanisms, such as interface roughness, potential fluctuations, or material defects. Their physical behavior is highly similar to that of Majorana modes, making it challenging to rule them out through simple theoretical models.

Experimental systems are often finite-sized (e.g., nanowires of limited length), which obscures the non-local properties of Majorana modes and makes them resemble localized Andreev bound states. So far, there is no conclusive experimental evidence directly demonstrating this non-locality. However, ongoing research is steadily approaching this goal, particularly in high-quality nanowire and quantum dot systems.

I don’t quite understand. The external magnetic field inherently breaks spin degeneracy, and SOC also breaks spin degeneracy. How does this work together?

This is an excellent question involving the respective roles of external magnetic fields and spin-orbit coupling (SOC) in realizing topological superconductors and Majorana zero modes. While both seem to break spin degeneracy, their mechanisms and effects are not identical. Together, SOC and the external magnetic field create a complementary effect, enabling the system to exhibit topological properties:

SOC Creates Momentum-Dependent Effective Magnetic Fields

SOC introduces an effective spin-dependent magnetic field during electron motion, with a direction related to electron momentum. This effective magnetic field does not require an actual magnetic field but arises from the interaction between momentum and lattice spin. For instance, in Rashba SOC: \[ \mathbf{B}_{\text{eff}} \propto \mathbf{k} \times \hat{z}, \] This effective field ties the spin orientation to the electron’s momentum, introducing spin-momentum locking and breaking spin degeneracy. This is a crucial prerequisite for the system to exhibit topological superconducting states.

The External Magnetic Field Adds Zeeman Splitting

Building on the effective field created by SOC, the Zeeman effect from an external magnetic field further breaks residual symmetries, enabling the topological phase transition:

  • Without an external magnetic field, SOC only provides a momentum-dependent effective field, and the system retains time-reversal symmetry (TRS).
  • The external magnetic field breaks TRS, opens a topological gap, and enables the system to transition from a trivial superconducting state to a topological superconducting state.

In a one-dimensional nanowire, SOC creates spin-split bands, while the external magnetic field further splits the bands, ultimately leading to the formation of a topological superconducting state.

Remind me of the essence of SOC. In atomic physics, I learned that it is the coupling of orbital angular momentum and spin angular momentum. But I’ve forgotten why they couple.

The fundamental cause of spin-orbit coupling (SOC) lies in relativistic effects. As electrons orbit the nucleus, their small mass and high speed result in the following characteristics due to relativistic effects: Electrons Perceive the Nuclear Electric Field as a “Magnetic Field”

  • In the atomic reference frame, electrons orbiting a stationary nucleus experience the nuclear electric field \(\mathbf{E}\).
  • However, in the electron’s reference frame, the electric field is Lorentz-transformed into an effective magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}_{\text{eff}}\): \[ \mathbf{B}_{\text{eff}} \propto \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{E}, \] This effective magnetic field is related to the electron’s motion and thus its orbital angular momentum \(\mathbf{L}\).

The intrinsic magnetic moment from the electron’s spin angular momentum couples with \(\mathbf{B}_{\text{eff}}\). This coupling arises from the interaction between the magnetic dipole moment and the magnetic field, with an energy form: \[ H_{\text{SOC}} \propto \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B}_{\text{eff}}. \] Since the effective magnetic field originates from the electron’s orbital motion, it can be described using orbital angular momentum \(\mathbf{L}\). Thus, spin-orbit coupling ultimately manifests as a coupling between \(\mathbf{S}\) and \(\mathbf{L}\): \[ H_{\text{SOC}} = \lambda \mathbf{L} \cdot \mathbf{S}, \] where \(\lambda\) represents the SOC strength.

SOC in Solid Crystals

In solid crystals, the physical origin of SOC extends from atomic SOC to the effects of the crystal lattice on electron motion. Electrons in solids experience electric fields generated by the ion cores and crystal potential. These fields, transformed via Lorentz transformation, also generate momentum-dependent effective magnetic fields.

Speaking of which, why does this repeated reflection occur? Is it related to the dissipationless nature of Cooper pairs in the superconductor?

If there is a potential barrier at the N-S interface (e.g., discontinuities in a quantum dot or nanowire), electrons approaching the interface may undergo multiple reflections. When an electron is reflected as a hole at the interface, the hole can be reflected back, regenerating an electron. This reciprocal conversion between electrons and holes leads to repeated reflections.

Dissipationless Nature of Cooper Pairs

The dissipationless transmission of Cooper pairs is key to Andreev reflection and the formation of Andreev bound states:

  • The superconducting gap restricts the conditions under which electrons can enter the superconductor. Low-energy electrons must enter indirectly through Andreev reflection.
  • When an electron enters the superconductor via Andreev reflection, it is not scattered or loses energy but remains stable through the Cooper pair mechanism.

This dissipationless property allows electrons and holes to repeatedly convert at the N-S interface without attenuation. The coherence of electrons and holes (ensured by superconducting properties) resembles resonance in sound, forming discrete bound states.

It’s strange. If the spin is perpendicular to the orbit, this term should be large. But this picture seems very common, yet we say strong SOC materials are rare.

You’ve pointed out an interesting apparent contradiction: if the spin is perpendicular to the orbital angular momentum, the coupling term \(\mathbf{L} \cdot \mathbf{S}\) should indeed be large. However, we also know that strong spin-orbit coupling (SOC) materials are relatively rare, which seems counterintuitive.

SOC arises from the effective magnetic field generated by an electron’s motion in the electric field of the atomic nucleus or lattice. Thus, the strength of SOC depends primarily on the electric field strength: \[ H_{\text{SOC}} \propto \frac{1}{c^2} (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{S}, \] In heavy elements, the nuclear charge \(Z\) is large, and electrons near the nucleus experience a very strong electric field, enhancing SOC: \[ H_{\text{SOC}} \sim Z^2. \] Despite the ubiquity of SOC, strong SOC materials are rare due to the following reasons:

Most Materials Have Low Atomic Numbers

SOC strength scales with \(Z^2\). Common light elements (e.g., silicon, carbon) exhibit relatively weak SOC effects. While heavy elements (e.g., lead, bismuth) have strong SOC, their occurrence is limited, making strong SOC materials rare.

Lattice Modulation of SOC

Even if an individual atom exhibits strong SOC, the lattice structure may suppress its contribution:

  • In high-symmetry lattices, spin directions are tightly constrained, reducing the effective SOC.
  • In inversion-symmetric lattices, SOC may be further weakened (e.g., the absence of Rashba effects in centrosymmetric crystals).

Electronic Distribution Characteristics

SOC strength also depends on the electronic orbital distribution:

  • In \(s\)-orbitals, the orbital angular momentum \(\mathbf{L}\) is zero, eliminating SOC entirely.
  • In \(p\)- and \(d\)-orbitals, \(\mathbf{L}\) is larger, but only certain materials allow \(\mathbf{L} \cdot \mathbf{S}\) to reach significant values.

While the geometric relationship of spin perpendicular to orbit is common, the following reasons explain why this doesn’t always lead to strong SOC:

  1. Quantum Nature of Electron Wavefunctions: In atoms, electron motion follows quantum wavefunction distributions. While the classical picture suggests "perpendicularity," quantum superposition and extension effects often reduce \(\mathbf{L} \cdot \mathbf{S}\) coupling.
  2. Suppression in Many Materials: In many materials, spin-orbit coupling is constrained by lattice symmetry and electronic filling.

Examples of Strong SOC Materials

  • Heavy metals: Such as bismuth (Bi) and lead (Pb), where high atomic numbers yield strong SOC effects.
  • Topological insulators: Like \(Bi_2Se_3\) and \(Bi_2Te_3\), where SOC drives topologically protected surface states.
  • Semiconductor heterostructures: Such as GaAs/InAs nanowires, where SOC manifests through Rashba effects.

Supplementary

Andreev Bound States

Let us first review the BdG equation in superconducting systems:

\[ \begin{pmatrix} H_0 & \Delta \\ \Delta^* & -H_0^* \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u \\ v \end{pmatrix} = E \begin{pmatrix} u \\ v \end{pmatrix} \]

The electron and hole wavefunctions at the interface satisfy continuity conditions and are subject to the following phase factors:

Phase Relationship in Andreev Reflection

When an electron is reflected as a hole, a phase factor \(\phi_A\) is introduced, originating from the Cooper pair condensate of the superconductor: \[ \phi_A = \arccos\left(\frac{E}{\Delta}\right) \]

Role of the Interface Barrier

The N-S interface is often modeled as a scattering barrier, causing the electron and hole to acquire additional reflection phases \(\phi_r\) and \(\phi_h\), respectively.

Condition for a Closed Path

To form bound states through the reflection path, the accumulated phase of the electron and hole must satisfy a quantization condition: \[ 2\phi_A + \phi_r + \phi_h = 2\pi n. \]

Analytical Solution for ABS Energy

In a simple model, the energy of Andreev bound states can be derived from the quantization condition: \[ E_n = \Delta \cos\left(\frac{\phi_r + \phi_h}{2} - \frac{\pi n}{2}\right). \]

Zero-Energy Condition (E = 0)

  1. When the interface barrier is symmetric (\(\phi_r = \phi_h\)), the energy can degenerate to zero.
  2. In special cases, system asymmetries (e.g., defects or impurities) may lead to \(\phi_r + \phi_h = \pi\), resulting in \(E = 0\) as a solution.

In finite-length one-dimensional nanowires, the coupling of electrons and holes may further adjust the phase conditions:

  • In sufficiently long systems (infinite-length limit), the coupling between Majorana modes and Andreev bound states weakens.
  • In finite-length systems, phase interference between electrons and holes more easily aligns the ABS energy to zero, forming a zero-bias conductance peak (ZBCP).

Localized States and Pseudo-Majorana Modes

Introducing local impurities may form a type of localized zero-energy state resembling "pseudo-Majorana modes," often caused by the ABS mechanism.

In tunneling experiments, the ZBCP due to Andreev bound states can be quantitatively described using the conductance formula: \[ G(V) \propto \frac{\Gamma^2}{E^2 + \Gamma^2}, \] where \(E \to 0\) leads to a sharp conductance peak, representing the ZBCP.

Kitaev Hamiltonian

The Kitaev chain model can be expressed as: \[ H = -\mu \sum_{i} c_i^\dagger c_i -t \sum_{i} \left( c_{i}^\dagger c_{i+1} + c_{i+1}^\dagger c_{i} \right) \\ + \Delta \sum_{i} \left( c_{i} c_{i+1} + c_{i+1}^\dagger c_{i}^\dagger \right). \]

By applying a Bogoliubov transformation, this model transforms into an effective superconducting system. The fermionic operators \(c_i\) and \(c_i^\dagger\) can be expressed in terms of Majorana operators: \[ \begin{aligned} c_i &= \frac{1}{2} (\gamma_{i,A} + i\gamma_{i,B}), \\ c_i^\dagger &= \frac{1}{2} (\gamma_{i,A} - i\gamma_{i,B}), \end{aligned} \] where the Majorana operators satisfy the commutation relations \(\{\gamma_{i,\alpha}, \gamma_{j,\beta}\} = 2\delta_{ij}\delta_{\alpha\beta}\) and the self-conjugacy condition \(\gamma_{i,\alpha}^\dagger = \gamma_{i,\alpha}\).

To analyze the system's topological properties, the Hamiltonian is transformed into momentum space. Using the Fourier transform: \[ \begin{aligned} c_j &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_k c_k e^{ikj}, \\ c_j^\dagger &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_k c_k^\dagger e^{-ikj}, \end{aligned} \] substituting yields the momentum-space Hamiltonian: \[ H = \sum_k \Psi_k^\dagger \mathcal{H}(k) \Psi_k, \] where \(\Psi_k = \begin{pmatrix} c_k \\ c_{-k}^\dagger \end{pmatrix}\) is the Nambu spinor, and \(\mathcal{H}(k)\) is the BdG Hamiltonian: \[ \mathcal{H}(k) = \begin{pmatrix} -\mu - 2t\cos k & 2i\Delta \sin k \\ -2i\Delta \sin k & \mu + 2t\cos k \end{pmatrix}. \] Diagonalizing the BdG Hamiltonian gives the energy spectrum: \[ E_k = \pm \sqrt{(\mu + 2t\cos k)^2 + (2\Delta \sin k)^2}. \]

  • When \(|\mu| < 2|t|\), the system has a gap and is in a topologically nontrivial phase.
  • When \(|\mu| > 2|t|\), the system is in a trivial phase.

Topological Invariant and Boundary States

The presence of a topological phase can be identified by calculating topological invariants such as the winding number: \[ w = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} dk \frac{d}{dk} \ln\det Q(k), \]

\[ Q(k) = \mathcal{H}(k) + iE_k I. \]

For the Kitaev chain, when \(|\mu| < 2|t|\), \(w = 1\)​, indicating a topologically nontrivial phase.

Assuming the chain has a finite length \(L\), the wavefunctions of the boundary states decay exponentially: \[ \psi_{\text{L}}(j) \sim e^{-j/\xi}, \quad \psi_{\text{R}}(j) \sim e^{-(L-j)/\xi}, \] In the topological phase, the Hamiltonian solutions allow for zero-energy states localized at the chain boundaries. These zero modes are constructed from combinations of Majorana operators, with boundary zero modes at both ends: \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_{\text{L}} &= \sum_j \psi_{\text{L}}(j) \gamma_{j,A}, \\ \gamma_{\text{R}} &= \sum_j \psi_{\text{R}}(j) \gamma_{j,B}. \end{aligned} \] These zero modes are Majorana zero modes, independent and topologically protected. Assuming chain length \(L\), the zero-mode wavefunctions approximately follow: \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_{\text{L}} &\sim e^{-i \theta} \sum_{n=1}^L \phi_n \gamma_{n,A}, \\ \gamma_{\text{R}} &\sim e^{i \theta} \sum_{n=1}^L \phi_n \gamma_{n,B}, \end{aligned} \] where \(\gamma_{\text{L}}\) and \(\gamma_{\text{R}}\) are localized at the left and right boundaries, respectively. The solved zero-mode wavefunctions decay exponentially and are localized at the two ends of the chain. These zero modes are independent and cannot be destroyed by local perturbations, thus forming a protected topological boundary state.

Summary of Majorana Zero Mode Origins

The Majorana zero modes in the topological phase arise from the special symmetries (e.g., particle-hole symmetry) of the system's Hamiltonian. They are boundary-localized states, protected and directly linked to the topological invariants. Mathematically, the existence of zero modes can be rigorously derived through fermion-Majorana transformations and topological analysis.
