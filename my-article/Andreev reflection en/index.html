Andreev reflection

Posted on 2025-01-23 Edited on 2025-02-05 In discussion Views:

中文版 Back to index

At the metal-superconductor interface, when an electron is reflected back from the normal metal, it generates a hole while forming a Cooper pair in the superconductor. This means that when electrons in the superconductor undergo reflection or scattering at the interface, the superconductor still retains its pairing mechanism. Does this imply that low-energy electrons become part of many Cooper pairs in the superconductor? What about electrons with energies higher than the gap?

The state of the incident electron is matched to the condensed state of the superconductor. This matching is achieved through the boundary conditions of momentum, energy, and phase continuity. When a hole is reflected, it "returns" part of the current to the normal metal, while the net current enters the superconductor through the formation of Cooper pairs.

Electrons with energy higher than the superconducting gap can directly enter the superconductor, but they do not form Cooper pairs; instead, they propagate as renormalized quasiparticles, existing as discrete energy states above the gap. Such quasiparticles are superpositions of electrons and holes, with their energy resulting from a combined contribution of kinetic energy and pairing potential.

For this electron, it becomes "superconducting." How does its nature change?

In normal metals, electrons are independent moving particles whose behavior is primarily governed by the Fermi-Dirac distribution and is influenced by scattering from the lattice or impurities. Once these electrons become Cooper pairs in the superconductor, they are no longer independent but are tightly correlated with another electron of opposite momentum and spin. Together, they form a quantum condensed state, manifesting as a macroscopic quantum phenomenon.

Why are such quasiparticles superpositions of electrons and holes? High-energy electrons entering the superconductor form quasiparticles—why do they create such superpositions? In contrast, low-energy electrons entering the superconductor form Cooper pairs, which are not electron-hole superpositions but rather a two-electron state.

In a superconductor, individual electron and hole states are no longer independent free states. Instead, they couple through the pairing mechanism within the superconducting energy gap, forming a new excitation state: the quasiparticle. The momentum of these quasiparticles is constrained by the boundary conditions of the superconducting condensate, but they still retain quasi-free propagation properties. This quasi-freedom arises because quasiparticles are not entirely decoupled from the coherent wavefunction of Cooper pairs but are superimposed upon it. Electrons and holes are no longer isolated particles; they are coupled via the superconducting gap. Quasiparticles are superpositions of electrons and holes, leading to fractional charge properties, and their energy states describe the rearrangement of electrons and holes in the superconductor.

\[ \gamma^\dagger_{k} = u_k c^\dagger_k - v_k c_{-k}, \]

represents the creation of an electron with momentum \(k\) and the annihilation of an electron with momentum \(-k\) (i.e., the creation of a hole with momentum \(-k\)). This superposition state reflects the non-classical behavior of single electrons (or holes) in the superconducting condensate.

Cooper pairs are bound states of two electrons with opposite momentum and energy lower than the superconducting gap. This two-electron state is a stable pairing structure, independent of single electron or single hole states:

\[ \Psi_{\text{Cooper Pair}} = c^\dagger_{k\uparrow} c^\dagger_{-k\downarrow}. \]

The superconducting pairing mechanism seems fascinating to me, as if it tightly connects two electrons moving in opposite directions and allows them to propagate in an even higher-order mechanism. The time scale of lattice deformation induces a local cloud of positive charge, creating an indirect electron-electron attraction.

Phonon frequencies are typically in the range of \(10^{12}-10^{13}\) Hz, corresponding to a time scale of \(0.1–1\) picoseconds. Within this recovery time, the interaction between two electrons is governed by quantum mechanics rather than being entirely overwhelmed by classical Coulomb repulsion. Due to the uncertainty principle, this short time scale allows the electrons to interact with the lattice, forming transient bound states whose stability is determined by the superconducting gap \(\Delta\). As long as the attraction between electrons overcomes energy fluctuations caused by thermal disturbance or other scattering, the bound state can persist. The balance between phonon lifetime (lag time) and electron velocity is a critical foundation of the Cooper pair theory.

However, pairing and superposition alone do not directly lead to the conclusion of a macroscopic wavefunction or dissipationless transport.

Your skepticism is very precise! Indeed, merely describing "pairing" and "superposition" does not directly lead to conclusions about the macroscopic wavefunction or dissipationless transport in superconductors. These phenomena need to be derived from higher-level theories. The pairing mechanism of Cooper pairs provides a starting point, but the core of superconductivity lies in the macroscopic condensation of all Cooper pairs.

Since electrons are identical particles, a large number of Cooper pairs can condense into a single macroscopic quantum state, forming an overall coherent wavefunction. In a superconductor, all Cooper pairs can be described by one macroscopic wavefunction:

\[ \Psi_{\text{macro}} = \prod_k \left(u_k + v_k c^\dagger_{k\uparrow} c^\dagger_{-k\downarrow}\right). \]

The phase of this wavefunction remains consistent across the entire superconductor. This phase coherence leads to many macroscopic phenomena:

  • Josephson effect: The tunneling current between superconductors is directly related to the phase difference.
  • Magnetic flux quantization: The magnetic flux in a superconductor is confined to quantized units (\(\Phi_0 = h/2e\)), a direct consequence of phase coherence.

Due to the overall coherence of the wavefunction, defects or impurities in the lattice cannot disrupt the motion of Cooper pairs (unless the energy exceeds \(\Delta\), sufficient to break the Cooper pairs themselves), leading to dissipationless transport.

I still don’t understand why this mechanism and propagation are dissipationless. So far, our discussion has not pinpointed what exactly makes it dissipationless. While we mentioned the coherence and wholeness of the wavefunction, we still haven’t specifically clarified why it is coherent and unbreakable.

An excellent question! To address why the superconducting mechanism leads to dissipationless transport and why Cooper pair propagation is not scattered or disrupted by impurities, we must delve deeper. This cannot simply be attributed to "coherence" alone; we need to explain why scattering fails and the deeper physical mechanism of coherence.

The coherence of the overall wavefunction originates from the spontaneous symmetry breaking of the superconductor (related to \(U(1)\) symmetry). The formation of the superconducting state lowers the free energy of the system, representing a spontaneous symmetry-breaking process. From a thermodynamic perspective, the superconducting condensate is a more stable state than the normal state. This stability arises from the collective behavior of the Cooper pairs in the system, rather than from the contributions of individual electrons or phonons.

In a normal metal, electrons are single particles, and their motion can be disrupted by local interactions, leading to scattering. In the superconducting condensate, the scattering effect on an individual Cooper pair is immediately absorbed by other Cooper pairs, preserving the overall coherence of the wavefunction. The phase of the superconducting wavefunction is a representation of a global quantum state, with extremely high "phase rigidity." If a local Cooper pair is perturbed (e.g., by scattering), the entire wavefunction adjusts its phase to compensate for the disturbance.

If coherence were broken, neither dissipationless transport nor the Meissner effect would exist. The phase rigidity ensures that the superconducting wavefunction cannot be easily disrupted, maintaining dissipationless current.

This point also puzzles me. The superconducting gap is a forbidden region for transmission. Why can it couple electrons and holes? Are the energies of electrons and holes, respectively, above and below the gap?

You’ve raised a very interesting and profound question! Indeed, the superconducting gap (\(\Delta\)) is a unique energy range that delineates the boundary between the superconducting state and the excited states. So, the key issue is: how can electrons and holes couple within this gap? Why can their energy ranges be related?

The coupling of electrons and holes arises because, in the superconductor, quasiparticle states are no longer pure electrons or holes but rather superpositions of both. While the superconducting gap constrains single-particle energy within a specific range (above \(\Delta\)), the superposition of electron and hole states still shares a common condensate wavefunction, allowing their momentum and energy to couple through the pairing potential (\(\Delta\)).

In a superconductor, the coupling of electrons and holes is not a straightforward image of “one electron above the gap and one hole below the gap.” Instead, the energy distribution of electrons and holes is jointly determined by the form of quasiparticle excitation. Near \(\Delta\), single electron and hole quantum states are no longer independent but form a mixed quasiparticle state (a superposition of electron and hole states). When electrons and holes couple, the system forms a lower-energy, more stable quasiparticle state through wavefunction mixing.

Has renormalization already occurred? How does it happen? It seems to be some kind of mathematical technique, but I still don’t understand how it manifests in our process.

A great question! Renormalization is not just a "mathematical technique" but also reflects how a physical system deals with complex interactions. In the context of superconductors, renormalization involves the formation of quasiparticles (superpositions of electron-hole states). Renormalization is a mathematical method used to handle the complexity of interactions in physical systems. For example, in interacting systems, the free state of a single particle is no longer a simple “bare particle” but becomes a “dressed particle” modified by interactions with its environment. Through renormalization, we introduce the concept of a “quasiparticle,” which is an effective description of a particle after interacting with its surroundings.

In superconductors, the superconducting pairing potential causes electrons and holes to couple. This means that the independent electron and hole states are no longer stable; they mix into a new superposition state. This renormalization process modifies the energy, momentum, and mass of the particles, leading to the formation of stable, effective excitation states.

I’ve been misunderstanding this the whole time. I always thought that electrons with \(k\) and \(-k\) were paired, but is it true that an electron with \(-k\) is actually a hole?

This is a very critical point you’ve touched upon! In the context of momentum-space matching, it is indeed easy to cause some misunderstanding. Let’s clarify this issue and explain why this perspective is crucial for understanding superconducting theory.

A hole with momentum \(-k\) can be understood as the absence of an electron with momentum \(-k\). This conversion of description is achieved through the concept of holes. Near the Fermi surface, an electron with momentum \(-k\) is equivalent to the dual state of an electron with momentum \(k\).

On the Fermi surface, the excitation states of electrons and holes are symmetric. An electron with momentum \(-k\) can be viewed as a “hole”: if an electron with momentum \(k\) disappears from the Fermi surface, this is described as the appearance of a hole at momentum \(-k\).

In momentum space, the wavefunctions of electrons and holes are mutually dual. This duality is reflected in the symmetry of the Fermi surface. Thus, in a superconducting system, the pairing mechanism between an electron with momentum \(k\) and an electron with momentum \(-k\) can naturally be reinterpreted from the perspective of electron-hole coupling.

The phonon-mediated electron-electron (hole) coupling process doesn’t sound “coherent” at all (e.g., inducing a positive charge cloud and attracting another electron). Yet, this process is so holistic and robust, even leading to so many strange phenomena and results.

You’ve pointed out a very intriguing and important question: The phonon-mediated electron-electron (or electron-hole) coupling process seems “local” and even “random,” yet it macroscopically exhibits extraordinary coherence and robustness, resulting in the peculiar phenomena observed in superconductors. The answer lies in the quantum mechanical collective effects and the nature of condensed matter systems.

Why does phonon-mediated electron-electron attraction seem incoherent?

  • Electron interactions via the lattice are fundamentally “local,” limited by the speed of phonon propagation and electron motion.
  • Noise and randomness introduced by lattice vibrations. Phonon frequencies and propagation characteristics are discrete, but phonons of different frequencies can couple to electrons of varying momenta, introducing “selectivity.”

Even though phonon-mediated electron-electron coupling appears susceptible to disturbances on a local scale, the entire superconducting system displays remarkable robustness. This robustness arises from:

  • Quantization of local interactions: The phonon-mediated electron-electron coupling is a quantum process that can extend across the entire system.
  • Global quantum coherence: The paired states’ wavefunction forms a unified macroscopic quantum state, averaging out local randomness and forming a stable condensate.

The interplay of these mechanisms ensures that, despite local fluctuations, the system remains globally coherent and stable, allowing the emergence of superconductivity.

Explain the phenomenon of subgap conductance enhancement in greater detail.

Andreev reflection allows current to pass through the metal-superconductor interface even when single-particle tunneling is forbidden. This provides an additional transmission channel for current, leading to enhanced conductance. In tunneling experiments, subgap conductance is often several times higher than the conductance of normal metal contacts.

In some cases, the interface can induce localized quantum states known as Andreev Bound States (ABS), which are directly related to Andreev reflection. Subgap conductance enhancement is a result of Andreev reflection prominently boosting conductance for electrons with energies below \(\Delta\). For high-energy quasiparticles (above \(\Delta\)), the conductance characteristics resemble normal metallic behavior, and the scattering properties of the quasiparticles become evident.

Isn’t this similar to some semiconductor devices, like diodes?

Diodes: Nonlinearity arises from classical \(pn\) junction barriers and diffusion processes.

Superconducting devices: Nonlinearity arises from quantum mechanical effects (Andreev reflection and the superconducting pairing potential).

While the physical mechanisms differ, these similarities can be leveraged for device design:

  • Andreev reflection effects at the superconductor-normal metal interface can mimic diode-like directional behavior by tuning the tunneling barrier or interface properties.
  • In certain superconductor-graphene heterostructures, this diode-like behavior is further amplified, allowing for the design of superconducting electronic devices.
  • Nonlinear properties of superconducting devices are widely used in high-sensitivity sensors (e.g., Josephson junctions or SQUIDs), which outperform traditional diode sensors in sensitivity. Similar to diodes, the nonlinearity of superconducting devices can be used to rectify and modulate superconducting electronic flow.

Under what circumstances are Andreev Bound States induced?

Andreev Bound States are localized quantum states that form at the superconductor-normal metal interface or in weak-link regions. The wavefunction amplitude of these states decays with distance from the interface, and the bound state energy depends on the scattering phase of the interface. Their formation relies on the coherent reflection of electrons and holes and the interference effect of multiple reflections.

  • Finite interfaces: For example, in superconductor-normal metal-superconductor (SNS) weak links, electrons and holes reflect between interfaces. If the reflection phase satisfies a standing wave condition, stable bound states form.
  • Energy spectrum: These bound states fall within the superconducting gap \(\Delta\) and do not enter the continuum spectrum.
  • Reflection probability: Higher reflection probability (e.g., low interface transmission) increases the likelihood of bound state formation. Conversely, in strong coupling, bound states may broaden or disappear.

For standard \(s\)-wave superconductors, the energy spectrum of Andreev Bound States is typically symmetric: \(E_{\text{ABS}} = \pm \Delta \cdot f(\phi)\). In \(p\)-wave superconductors or systems with strong spin-orbit coupling, Andreev Bound States may evolve into zero-energy states, exhibiting non-Abelian statistics (e.g., Majorana modes).

  • Josephson junctions: In these, the energy of Andreev Bound States directly depends on the phase difference between two superconductors, changing periodically with the phase difference \(\phi\):

\[ E_{\text{ABS}} = \pm \Delta \sqrt{1 - \tau \sin^2(\phi / 2)}, \]

where \(\tau\) is the transmission coefficient. The existence of Andreev Bound States can be observed through tunneling spectroscopy, where their energy corresponds to peaks in tunneling conductance. In quantum dot-superconductor systems, nonlinear conductance behavior can reflect the presence of Andreev Bound States.

Supplementary

Derivation Starting from the Hamiltonian

The behavior of electrons in superconductors can be described using the BCS Hamiltonian: \[ H = \sum_k (\epsilon_k - \mu) c^\dagger_k c_k + \sum_{k,k'} V_{k,k'} c^\dagger_{k\uparrow} c^\dagger_{-k\downarrow} c_{-k'\downarrow} c_{k'\uparrow}, \]

The BCS ground state is the condensate of Cooper pairs, described by the wavefunction \(\Psi_{\text{BCS}}\). Its key feature is macroscopic coherence: \[ \Psi_{\text{BCS}} = \prod_k \left(u_k + v_k c^\dagger_{k\uparrow} c^\dagger_{-k\downarrow}\right) |0\rangle. \]

Considering the coupling between electrons and holes, a simplified effective Hamiltonian can be introduced, which can be diagonalized to calculate the eigenvalues of the quasiparticle energies: \[ H_{\text{eff}} = \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_k - \mu & \Delta \\ \Delta & -(\epsilon_k - \mu) \end{pmatrix}. \]

Solving for the eigenvalues of this matrix, we obtain the quasiparticle excitation energies in the superconducting condensate: \[ E_k = \sqrt{(\epsilon_k - \mu)^2 + \Delta^2}. \]

Since quasiparticles are superpositions of electrons and holes, their energy must account for both the kinetic energy and the pairing potential. The quadratic summation reflects the independent contributions of these two effects.

  • For quasiparticles near the Fermi surface \(|\epsilon_k - \mu| \approx 0\), the energy approaches \(\Delta\).
  • For high-momentum states \(|\epsilon_k - \mu| \gg \Delta|\), the energy approximates the free-particle behavior of classical electrons or holes.

The formation of quasiparticle states implies that the energies of electrons and holes are effectively "mixed" into the same range, rather than being separated into "above" or "below the gap."

Renormalization in Superconducting Theory

The initial system describes the behavior of free electrons: \[ H_0 = \sum_k \epsilon_k c_k^\dagger c_k. \]

The coupling between electrons and phonons is described by the interaction Hamiltonian: \[ H_{\text{int}} = \sum_{k,k'} g_{k,k'} c_k^\dagger c_{k'} (a_q + a_q^\dagger). \]

Electron-phonon interactions modify the self-energy \(\Sigma(k, \omega)\) of the electrons, including all virtual processes mediated by phonons, thereby altering the mass and energy of the electrons. Using Feynman diagram techniques, the self-energy \(\Sigma(k, \omega)\) encapsulates all phonon-mediated virtual processes: \[ G_{\text{eff}}(k, \omega) = \frac{1}{\omega - \epsilon_k - \Sigma(k, \omega)}. \] Here, \(\Sigma(k, \omega)\) represents the renormalized energy correction, directly determining the effective mass and energy of the quasiparticles.

Electron-phonon interactions introduce an effective attractive potential near the Fermi surface: \(V_{\text{eff}} = -\lambda\). This attractive potential causes electrons with momenta \((k, -k)\) to form Cooper pairs, ultimately leading to the formation of a superconducting condensate on a global scale. By diagonalizing the effective Hamiltonian, the wavefunctions and energies of the quasiparticles can be obtained.

Current-Phase Relation: The Josephson Effect

Each Cooper pair in a superconductor shares a macroscopic wavefunction: \[ \Psi = |\Psi| e^{i\phi}. \]

The phase difference \(\Delta\phi = \phi_1 - \phi_2\) between two superconductors determines the magnitude and direction of the current. The DC Josephson current \(I_s\) is related to the phase difference by: \[ I_s = I_c \sin(\Delta\phi), \]

where the current’s dependence on \(\sin(\Delta\phi)\) is a typical quantum interference phenomenon, directly relying on the stability of the wavefunction phase. This interference indicates that the wavefunctions of the two superconductors remain correlated, reflecting the global coherence of the superconducting condensate. Even across a thin connecting layer, the phases of the wavefunctions can remain linked. If the relative phase difference \(\Delta\phi\) between the wavefunctions of the two superconductors can be stably defined, the superconducting current in the weak link can flow indefinitely without resistance.

In experiments, the current-phase relation can be measured by adjusting the external current or magnetic field applied to the Josephson junction. Typical experiments display periodic current oscillations, fully determined by \(\sin(\Delta\phi)\). When the Josephson junction forms a closed loop (SQUID), the magnetic flux within the loop is quantized: \[ \Phi = n\Phi_0. \] This quantization of magnetic flux directly depends on the phase consistency of the wavefunction. The quantum interference effect of the phase leads to periodic variations in current under a magnetic field, manifesting as a Fraunhofer pattern.
