equilibrium states corresponding to a known temperature can be effectively captured using the framework of quantum statistical mechanics

how these states can be reached through local dynamics that follows microscopic laws

microscopic description--familiar ensembles of statistical mechanics

how one might grasp the actual dynamics of quantum phase transitions

local quantum many-body systems with finite-range interactions, ubiquitous in condensed-matter physics. described by some interaction graph, with physical constituents at the vertices. nearest-neighbour interactions, \(h_i\)​ acts non-trivially on finitely many sites. Paradigmatic example, Bose-Hubbard model. \[ H=J\sum_{j,k}(b_j^\dagger b_k+b_k^\dagger b_j)+U\sum_j n_j(n_j-1) \] closed local quantum many-body systems out of equilibrium

Equilibration after quenches

how thermodynamics emerges from microscopic quantum dynamics

a very clear setting: the one emerging from a sudden global quench

ground state->instantaneously alters the system's parameters globally and consider many-body unitary time evolution under some local Hamiltonian \(H\)

expectation values of observables \(A\) at later times \[ \langle A(t)\rangle=\text{Tr}(e^{-iHt}\rho_0e^{iHt}A) \] to what extent and for what times the situation can be described by a suitable equilibrium ensemble

the dynamics is time-reversal invariant

finite system, how and in what sense equilibrium can be reached dynamically

  • such systems generically relax and equilibrate: despite the unitary dynamics, following transient non-equilibrium dynamics, expectation values of many observables equilibrate. This is specifically true for local observables, which are supported only on a small number of sites. The apparent long-time equilibrium state has to be equal to the time average. This state is the maximum entropy state, holding all constants of motion fixed. In the case of non-degenerate eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian, this ensemble is also called the diagonal ensemble.

    reminiscent of a dynamical Jaynes' principle: a many-body system is pushed out of equilibrium and follows unitary dynamics

    integrable non-interacting limit. equilibration during intervals. local observables \(|\langle A(t)\rangle-Tr(A)|\) arbitrarily small after a known initial relaxation time, and remain until a recurrence time that grows linearly with system size. assume the otherwise arbitrary initial state has suitably decaying correlations

    non-commutative central limit theorems, Lieb-Robinson bounds (linear scaling of recurrence time, excitations need to travel through the whole system and reflected at the boundary)

    no locality structure is available: one can still show equilibration on average for all Hamiltonians that have non-degenerate energy gaps, one can bound the time average by a quantity that is usually expected to scale as one over the square root of the Hilbert space dimension of the total system. This means that such out-of -equilibrium quantum systems seem to be relaxed for most times, even if by that statement alone no information on timescales can be deduced. describe the size of the time fluctuation after relaxation

    Ramps or sweeps, during which the Hamiltonian is changed according to some schedule, are of interest in studies of dynamics of quantum phase transitions. geometry, time-dependent periodic driving, pump-probe type settings

  • Thermalization: equilibration towards a state that is close to being indistinguishable from a thermal equilibrium state

    thermodynamics in describing large-scale systems indicates that often equilibrium values can be described by a few parameters, such as the global temperature and particle number

    mechanism: ETH, conjectures that sufficiently complex quantum systems have eigenstates that are indistinguishable from thermal states with the same average energy. ETH and its breakdown in integrable systems

    thermalization in weakly interacting systems under stronger conditions on the initial state. dynamical thermalization of local expectation values to those in the global thermal state has been shown under stronger conditions, most notably translation invariance and the existence of unique Gibbs state. Effective thermalization under unitary quantum dynamics can also be made plausible under conditions of classical chaos. relative thermalization focuses on the decoupling of a subsystem from a reference system

    there is so far no compelling proof that globally quenched non-integrable systems dynamically thermalize under local unitary many-body dynamics

    typicality arguments. rather than following the dynamical evolution of a system, one tries to justify the applicability of ensembles by showing that most states drawn randomly according to some measure have the same properties as some appropriate ensemble

    If a quantum system is integrable, in the sense that it has sufficiently many local conserved quantities, one should not expect the system to thermalize. these constants of motion prohibit full thermalization to the canonical ensemble. equilibrate to the maximum entropy state, generalized Gibbs ensemble. The explicit construction of GGE for general interacting integrable models remains an open problem

    pre-thermalization, almost-integrable systems, continuous systems of coupled Bose-Einstein condensates. Specially, in a close-to-free model, there's an initial build-up of correlations where quasi-particles are formed, where as actual thermalization occurs on much longer timescales

  • ‘Light cones’ and entanglement dynamics

    understanding of the nonequilibrium dynamics of local Hamiltonian models

    That is to say, information propagation outside a `light cone' is exponentially suppressed. It immediately follows that correlation functions can grow significantly in time only inside the light cone.

    Although these bounds are not necessarily tight, the picture that the local dynamics leads to excitations travelling through the system with velocity v offers much explanatory potential for nonequilibrium dynamics

    Many-body states are said to satisfy an area law for the entanglement entropy, if for a subset of sites the latter scales only like the boundary area of the subset rather than its volume. It has been shown that if initially such a law holds, this will remain true for longer times. Yet, at the same time, the prefactor of this area law is expected to grow exponentially

    However, all numerical tools share the feature that they are limited to short times, which is in line with arguments using complexity theory, which provide evidence that generic quantum long-time dynamics cannot be efficiently classically simulated

  • Transport

    simple paradigmatic models such as the Heisenberg spin chain or Hubbard-type models. In these models, transport is expected to lead to equilibration, as it allows the system to locally forget its precise initial configuration

    Two of the key aims are to distinguish between diffusive and ballistic transport and to understand the limitations of linear response theory

  • Absence of thermalization and many-body localization

    defects, random potentials. non-interacting fermions in one dimension or, equivalently, via Jordan Wigner transformation, for free spin chains, Anderson's result gives strong localization of the fermionic modes. These systems thus fail to serve as their own heat bath, and transport is strongly suppressed

    For interacting many-body systems, the effects of disorder are still much less clear, despite great efforts of research in many-body localization. Treating disorder as a perturbation, and starting from the Fock space of Slater determinants, a key step in the understanding was to develop an intuition of localization in Fock space

    Interestingly, for out-of-equilibrium situations, both the support of time-evolved local observables as well as the entanglement entropy are numerically found to grow logarithmically in the interacting case, and are thus unbounded

    In several recent studies, localization eects due to disorder have been associated with a lack of entanglement in many energy eigenstates, or alternatively only in those below a so-called mobility edge

    The localization of eigenstates leads to a clear violation of the ETH and connects to an absence of thermalization eects for localizing many-body systems and the existence of local constants of motion in these models

  • Dynamics of quantum phase transitions

    dynamical signatures of phase transitions. A key theoretical model for these transitions, based on the adiabatic theorem, has been provided by Kibble and Zurek for the important case of moving from a gapped phase into criticality.

    For strongly correlated systems in realistic experimental settings, the dynamics of quantum phase transitions is more elusive and there are indications that the complexity of the dynamics cannot fully be captured by employing simple scaling arguments based only on the critical exponents of the model

  • Quantum simulations

    A particularly exciting perspective arises from the idea that experimentally controlled quantum many-body systems constitute instances of analogue quantum simulators95 overcoming these limitations. These devices mimic natural interacting quantum many-body systems by reconstructing their Hamiltonian, but now under precisely controlled conditions.

    Ultracold atoms in optical lattices. Following up on that, the equilibration dynamics of such systems after quenches, the light-cone-like spreading of correlations, and the expansion dynamics of interacting bosons in homogeneous lattices in one and two dimensions have been explored

    Ultracold fermionic atoms. By making use of Feshbach resonances, appropriate periodic driving, and superlattices generated by additional optical lattices with half the wavelength, sophisticated quantum simulations are conceivable for example, the simulation of lattice gauge theories

  • Periodically driven systems

    The eigenstates of such systems are usually constantly changing in time, but the long-time dynamics can be inferred from their Floquet operator

    The effective Hamiltonian can be tuned by choosing an appropriate driving schedule with great flexibility concerning the model parameters. The synchronization of the motion of periodically driven systems with the drive allows the description of the long-time behaviour by a periodic ensemble

  • Perspectives for closed and open systems

    The AdS/CFT-correspondence also contributes a novel tool to study strongly correlated quantum systems out of equilibrium

    Although it is understood under which conditions many-body systems equilibrate, it is far from clear on what timescales they do so. The main reason for this is that the role of locality for equilibration and thermalization is, at least from a rigorous perspective, far from being understood

    A better understanding of the timescales of equilibration seems crucial, as it is necessary to know whether the relevant physics is captured by the long-time equilibrium state or happens in a possibly markedly dierent non-equilibrium or quasi-stationary regime. The problem of many-body localization has recently become a focus of attention, and is developing rapidly, even if a unifying framework of the various approaches still seems missing

    a research area that deserves special attention is based on the idea that noise and dissipation are not necessarily detrimental to attempts to prepare and preserve interesting states of matter. In fact, such preparations can be facilitated by suitably engineered local Markovian noise, which can be rigorously captured in terms of the Liouvillian time evolution of open quantum systems.
