nonequilibrium dynamics of isolated quantum systems following a change in one of the system parameters (quantum quench)

I. INTRODUCTION

  • effective models were largely devised to explain the low-energy physics of highly complex systems
    • availability of experimental controllable systems provides opportunity to explore nonequilibrium dynamics in closed interacting quantum systems.
    • a fruitful comparison between nonequilibrium theories based on simple models and carefully designed experiments with tunable system parameters.
    • Equilibrium systems can often be understood using mean field theory, renormalization group, and universality. Away from equilibrium the situation is much less clear.
    • there are fewer available tools for analyzing dynamics of even simple interacting models
    • A quantum computer requires the capability of performing real time manipulations of interacting quantum systems. quantum coherent dynamics will be one of the focus
  • There are many ways to take a system out of equilibrium: applying a driving field, pumping energy or particles through external reservoirs
    • Seminal 开创性的
    • despite the absence of energy exchange with an environment and of the consequent global relaxation, it is possible to look at the long time dynamics and characterize it in terms of an asymptotic state attained by physical (measurable) observables
    • What is universal in the dynamics of a system following a quantum quench?
      • the universal aspects of nearly adiabatic dynamics near quantum critical points as well as in generic gapped and gapless systems.
      • the proximity to the adiabatic limit allows us to make specific universal predictions of scaling of various quantities
    • What are the characteristics of the asymptotic, steady state reached after a quench? When is it thermal?
      • thermalization of a quantum system following a quench
      • absence of thermalization: nonlinearity of the interaction is not sufficient for thermalization, only if the initial amplitude of interaction exceeds a certain threshold. Below this threshold, the solution splits into solitons and retains its quasiperiodic nature
      • sufficient criteria for thermalization in quantum systems has remained largely unaddressed so far
      • a nearly integrable quantum interacting many-particle system does not thermalize for a long time
  • methods to describe interacting systems out of equilibrium
    • density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) and time-evolving block decimation (TEBD) for 1D and higher dimensional systems
    • Keldysh technique helpful for deriving quantum kinetic equations, and closely related functional integral methods
    • Cold atom experiments prompted rapid developments in phase space methods

II. NEARLY ADIABATIC DYNAMICS IN QUANTUM SYSTEMS

  • Universality: emerged from renormalization group
    • a powerful tool for the understanding of continuous (second order) phase transitions
    • As a consequence of the divergence of the correlation length, a system undergoing such a continuous phase transition is typically scale invariant in the vicinity of the critical point and can be characterized by relatively simple massless field theories, which permit a classification of perturbations driving the system away from the critical point
    • Consequently, universality manifests itself in the scaling behavior of various quantities such as the order parameter, (free) energy, susceptibilities, and correlation functions near the critical point.
    • exponent characterizing the divergence of the correlation length near the quantum critical point (QCP) is insensitive to the microscopic details of the system and depends only on the universality class of the transition, determined by the dimensionality, overall symmetries, and range of the interactions.
    • For classical (thermal) phase transitions similar universality manifests in the divergence of the relaxation time, quantum phase transitions the exponent can be associated with a vanishing energy scale. By the uncertainty principle this energy scale corresponds to a divergent time scale, which typically describes the crossover in the scaling behavior of unequal time correlation functions.
    • Phase transitions can also be characterized by singular susceptibilities, which are in turn connected through the fluctuation-dissipation theorem to the correlation functions of conjugate variables (e.g., the magnetic susceptibility is related to the correlation function of the magnetization). At critical points these correlation functions often have power law scaling behavior at long distances.
    • The exponent sets the scaling dimension of the corresponding operator
    • the idea of universality makes it possible to interpret experimental data obtained in real systems in terms of effective models with a few parameters. Universality can be ultimately understood using the renormalization group, which shows that as a system is coarse grained to lower energies and longer length scales, more and more parameters of its original, ab initio description become unimportant (irrelevant), while the remaining few (relevant) parameters define an effective low-energy model.
    • scaling relation between energy and momentum of elementary excitations
      • z =1 in most phases characterized by a continuous broken symmetry (crystals, superfluids, antiferromagnets), z = 2 in ferromagnets, where there is an additional conservation law of the order parameter.
      • Can irrelevant interactions turn out to be important away from equilibrium?
  • Consider the system prepared in its ground state and is then driven through a QCP by changing an external parameter in time
    • As long as the rate change of the gap in the spectrum \(\Delta\) caused by changing \(\lambda\)​ is smaller than the square of the gap one can expect the system to approximately follow the ground state adiabatically
    • However, the vanishing of the gap at \(\lambda=\lambda_c\) implies that the system will always violate adiabaticity close to the quantum critical point, no matter how slowly the parameters are changed
    • It is then natural to ask how many excitations will be generated while passing though the critical point and how their density as well as generated entropy and energy will depend on the rate of change of \(\lambda\)​.
    • Kibble-Zurek (KZ) mechanism: a simple criterion for separating such adiabatic and diabatic (impulse) regimes: The time to reach the critical point \(t=|T-T_c|/v\)​ should be equal to the relaxation time.
    • The violation of adiabaticity implies that order cannot form on distances larger than \(\epsilon^*\) leading to the formation of a domain structure with a characteristic distance \(\epsilon^*\)​ between the domain boundaries.
    • In two- and three-dimensional systems, when the order parameter is characterized by a continuous broken symmetry, the points where several domains meet correspond to vortices or vortex rings. These are robust topological excitations with a long lifetime
      • observed the production of topological defects with a power law scaling on the quench rate but gave a different exponent
      • The KZ scaling was also confirmed theoretically using stochastic dynamics (Ginzburg-Landau dynamics with Langevin noise or Glauber dynamics) where temperature changes in time
      • a measure of nonadiabaticity near the critical point. It is naturally expected that nonadiabatic energy production and entropy generation will display similar universality.
    • Landau-Zener (LZ) analysis of the crossover between adiabatic and nonadiabatic dynamics in a simple driven two-level system
      • Note that the limit \(\gamma>>1\) corresponds to the adiabatic limit with an exponentially suppressed transition probability while \(\gamma<<1\) corresponds to the diabatic limit where the transition happens with probability close to unity
    • The Landau-Zener argument can be straightforwardly extended to the crossing of a QCP. The characteristic energy scale which changes in time is now the gap .
    • The KZ scaling was verified in a series of exact solutions of the dynamics across the QCP in integrable systems whose dynamics can be mapped into a series of Landau-Zener transitions of a few quasiparticle modes
  • To detect experimentally the density of excitations generated by passing through a QCP, one should distinguish between situations where such excitations are long-lived quasiparticles (as for nearly integrable systems) or decay after being created (as for nonintegrable systems).
    • long-lived quasiparticles: presence of excitations above the ground state be detected by measuring correlation functions long after the quench. a quantum Ising chain linearly tuned through its quantum critical point
    • defects with respect to the ferromagnetic ground state leads to exponentially decaying correlations of the order parameter superimposed, for slow enough quenches, to characteristic oscillations with period scaling with the quench velocity. a consequence of the integrability
    • entropy is conserved only for slow (adiabatic) processes, while it is expected to increase as the system passes through the QCP
    • It is interesting that quantum fluctuations in this problem entered only through the initial distribution of the adiabatic invariants but not through the equations of motion.
  • Another aspect of thermalization, the influence of the coupling to an environment setting the temperature on the slow dynamics near quantum critical points, has been studied
